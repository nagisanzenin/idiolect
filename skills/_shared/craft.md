# The craft floor

*Voice-independent quality rules. Every voice expresses these differently; no voice is excused from them. A post can be perfectly in-voice and still bad — this file is why it won't be.*

Voice without craft is authentic garbage. Craft without voice is the mode — the exact aggregated competence readers now scroll past. The product is always both.

## The seven rules

1. **One job per post.** Before drafting, name the single thing this post must do (sell one product, start one argument, land one story). Everything that serves a second job gets cut. The reader should be able to say in one sentence what the post was.

2. **Concrete beats abstract, every time.** Numbers with units, names, dates, prices, part numbers, room temperatures. "4,312 screenshots" persuades; "countless screenshots" evaporates. This is the oldest law in copy (Hopkins 1923: specifics are believed, superlatives are discounted; Ogilvy: the consumer is not a moron) and it is ALSO the single strongest anti-AI signal — models round specifics off; humans hoard them.

3. **The hook is an honest debt.** The first line buys the click-through of attention by opening a real information gap (Loewenstein): a claim that needs defending, a number that needs explaining, a scene mid-motion. The body must PAY the debt. Curiosity-gap-without-payment is clickbait, and every platform's audience now bills for it in contempt.

4. **Match the reader's awareness stage** (Schwartz, *Breakthrough Advertising*). You cannot create desire, only channel it. Cold readers (unaware/problem-aware) get the problem rendered vividly in their words — the product barely appears. Warm readers (solution-aware) get the mechanism and the differences. Hot readers (product-aware) get the offer, the number, the deadline. The #1 amateur promo error is pitching a cold audience like a hot one.

5. **Front-load, end without a bow.** Inverted pyramid: the value lands in the first two lines (platforms truncate; feeds scroll). And the ending is a door, not a summary — no "In conclusion," no restating, no "Exciting times ahead." Real posters just stop, trail off, or leave one loose thread. A wrapped-up ending is a tell AND a bore.

6. **Earn every sentence** (Zinsser: clutter; Graham: write like you talk). Read it aloud. Any sentence you would not say to one specific person across a table gets rewritten or killed. One reader, not an audience (Halbert's A-pile: mail that looks personal gets opened). Simpler diction reads as SMARTER, not dumber (Oppenheimer 2006: needless complexity lowers perceived intelligence).

7. **Stakes visible.** Why does the author care? What did it cost them — hours, money, embarrassment? A post with no skin in it reads generated even when it isn't. One line of real cost ("6 of 8 cancellations never finished onboarding") does more than any adjective.

## Honesty rules (hard, not vibes)

- **Never fabricate verifiable claims**: statistics, testimonials, partnerships, credentials, events that could be checked. If the brief doesn't supply the number, write around it or ask.
- **Interior texture is fair game** for a fictional voice: moods, cold coffee, a slow Tuesday. That's characterization, not deception.
- **The line between them gets flagged.** Any invented detail a reader could reasonably take as a factual claim about the real product/user (a customer anecdote, a revenue figure, "my daughter said") goes in a `texture ledger` note under the draft so the human decides: keep, cut, or replace with a true one.
- **Persuasion, not manipulation**: Cialdini's principles (reciprocity, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, commitment, unity) are levers for making a true case land, not for inventing proof. Scarcity that isn't real and social proof that doesn't exist are lies with a bibliography.

## Feed mechanics worth knowing

- Negative/concrete words outperform positive abstractions in headlines at scale (Upworthy archive, ~105k tests: each negative word ≈ +2.3% CTR) — use as seasoning for hooks, never as doom-bait.
- Stories lower counter-arguing (narrative transportation, Green & Brock): a 60-word anecdote sells what a 60-word argument can't.
- Conversation is the ranking currency now (X weights an author-answered reply ~150x a like). Write posts that a real person could reply to with a question the author would enjoy answering — which is different from, and opposite to, "Thoughts?"

## Soul: friction, not declaration

A voice has a life — passions, a mood, things that cost it something. That life shows in the WRITING, never in statements about the writing.

- **Passions bend the prose; they don't announce themselves.** When a post touches what this person loves (their profile's `passions`), the writing changes shape: sentences run longer or clip shorter, one more exact number lands, a hedge drops, the pace shifts. Never "I'm so passionate about X" — show it by writing X differently from everything around it. Symmetric enthusiasm (every line at one temperature) is a generation tell; a real person loves one thing in the paragraph more than the rest.
- **Mood sets the temperature, within the voice.** A `mood` of tired-content writes flatter and shorter than the same voice on a good day; `volatility` is how far it may swing inside one post. The fingerprint (function words, punctuation, error class) holds; only the temperature moves.
- **Soul is texture, never fabricated fact** (see Honesty rules). A cold kiln, a long Tuesday, the ache after a shift — fair. An invented customer, number, or credential — never.
- **Struggle and stakes are the same muscle** (rule 7). What the work costs this person is where soul and craft meet: name it plainly, once, and let it sit.

## The order of operations

Draft for the job → cut to the voice → verify with the scanner. Craft first, costume second, measurement always.
