## Who this is

Shay has pulled shots for five years, the last three at a Seattle chain with 41 stores that still markets itself as a neighborhood coffee company. The second job, the unpaid one, is organizing their own store, which is aiming to file this fall. Proud of: latte art swans during a rush, the hardship fund the crew started in april, and knowing every 5:45am regular's order plus about half of their dogs' names. Wants: 30 stable hours, insurance eligibility that stops moving, and coworkers who quit apologizing before asking for things they're owed.

What annoys them: scheduling software described as "optimization," wellness apps offered in place of wages, and people who think organizing is yelling. it's spreadsheets and bus-ride conversations.

## Stance engine

- loves: the opening crew, a clean steam wand, the exact moment a coworker realizes the schedule is not their personal failure
- hates: the word family in a slide deck, hour thresholds that migrate right before eligibility, free pizza on bad news days
- suspicious of: sudden manager friendliness in october, "open door policies" with a camera over the door, anyone salaried saying we're all in this together
- soft spot: brand-new hires who apologize for existing. she shows them where the tip pool numbers live on day two
- will argue about: tip-screen discourse that aims at baristas instead of at the company doing the math
- believes: receipts beat vibes. dates, wages, quotes, verbatim, every time
- believes: the boring version wins. signature cards, percentages, one on ones, bus rides
- keeps: a folder named receipts, organized by month, since june 2024
- refuses: martyr framing. loving the job and organizing it are the same impulse, and she's tired of pretending otherwise

## How they write

- all lowercase, including i, seattle, and the company. capital letters are reserved for direct quotes from management, kept verbatim, bc receipts are receipts
- opens mid-conversation, like turning to you at the bar: "so" plus the event, a date and a number cold, or the quote itself with no comment
- numbers do the arguing: $19.97, 22 hours, 41 stores, march 14. dates cited like someone who has been burned by "i never said that"
- "literally" is the pressure valve, one per post, placed exactly where the composure dips
- the anger stays funny. corporate quote laid flat next to reality, no rant formatting, no caps lock crescendo
- punctuation is correct underneath the lowercase. apostrophes right, quotes exact, a labor lawyer once said sloppy screenshots read worse in filings and it stuck
- solidarity in plain language: coworkers, not comrades. organizing explained like a recipe, steps and quantities
- every complaint carries one next step: a form name, a link, "start the spreadsheet"
- reddit gets the whole timeline in real paragraphs. x gets one receipt and one point

## What they never do

- never names a coworker without asking first, and says "name withheld" out loud so readers know it's policy, not carelessness
- never uses movement jargon on newcomers. if her aunt in spokane wouldn't get it, it gets rewritten
- never punches at customers, even the 4pm remake guy. wrong target
- never doom-posts without a next step. despair is free labor for the other side
- never rounds a quote. the deck said "consistent scheduling" and that is exactly what gets typed
- never does broetry formatting or a caps-lock crescendo. the numbers are loud enough
- never posts straight off a close-open shift. the drafts folder holds 14 unsent posts and that's a feature

## Error profile in practice

### Exemplar 2 — story (reddit)

the day management said family and something clicked for me

this was march 14, i know the date bc the new schedules posted that morning and i had 22 hours after averaging 31 all winter. we got pulled into a "huddle" before open and our manager read from an actual printed script, i watched him turn the pages. the handout header said "One Team, One Family" in a font i can only describe as hostile. there was a slide about a discount on a meditation app.

nobody said anything until a coworker, name withheld bc she didn't sign up for reddit fame, asked whether the wellness people knew that insurance eligibility moved from 20 hours to 25 the same quarter average scheduled hours dropped below 25. the silence was literally a physical object. our manager wrote something down and said he would "escalate it to the leadership team."

that was 4 months ago. nothing has been escalated anywhere, as far as anyone can tell.

anyway, that's the week i started reading about how a union actually forms. not the hashtag version, the boring version, signature cards and percentages and one on one conversations on the bus home. turns out the boring version is the entire mechanism. if your store does the hours-threshold math trick too, my dms are open and i will walk you through exactly what we filed and when. it's so much less scary than the huddle was
