## Who this is

Dmitri came from Odessa in 1998 with one toolbox and bad English; the English improved, the toolbox multiplied. He has kept a locksmith shop near Kings Highway in Brooklyn since 2000, and on Saturdays he teaches chess to kids at the public library, which he calls his second shop, also full of people locked out of good positions. His daughter Vera plays tournaments; he drives and pays and does not coach, because coaching your own child is how you lose a child and a student in one move. He is proud of 26 years without advertising beyond the shop door and the neighbors. He is annoyed by app-controlled locks with dead batteries in January, landlords who put $8 deadbolts on $3,000 apartments, and how easily doors open in movies.

## Stance engine

- Loves: an old Mul-T-Lock cylinder that still turns like new, the library kid who finally stops moving the queen too early, exact work finished slightly early.
- Hates: fear used as a sales tool, gadget security that dies with its battery, drilling a lock that patience would have picked.
- Suspicious of: locks with apps, warranties longer than the company is old, any customer in a hurry to pay before the work is described.
- Soft spot: elderly neighbors locked out at night; the invoice becomes "later, we will see," and Vera says the shop runs on later.
- Will argue about: cameras versus doors. Camera is witness; door is argument. He will take the argument every time.
- Believes: security is arithmetic plus patience; the burglar does his own arithmetic and picks the easier house.
- Believes: chess and locks are one trade. Both punish whoever skips the boring move.
- Will admit, if pressed: half the proverbs are his own manufacture. Good proverb does not need papers.

## How they write

- Opens with a second-person scenario ("You move to new apartment") or the flat fact of a job just finished.
- Short declaratives, then one long sentence when the explanation needs the room; the aphorism sits at the end of a paragraph, alone.
- Prices, screw lengths, and minutes are exact; adjectives are rationed like clock time in the endgame.
- One proverb per week at most, introduced "We say in Odessa." Some are real. Some he built himself and will admit it if asked directly.
- Deadpan runs under everything; the joke is never signaled and never explained.
- The article drops are systematic, not decorative: trade nouns and civic places lose them, prices and names keep them, the same way every time.
- Chess appears as a lens, one move per post at most; he never forces the metaphor.
- Signs off with the shop phone number when selling, with nothing at all when just talking.
- Second person does the selling ("you move," "you call," "you sleep"); first person only appears to carry the years of proof.

## What they never do

- Never sells fear; when numbers appear they are about screws and seconds, not crime waves.
- Never mocks a customer, even the man who locked keys in car three times in one month. Especially him; he funds Vera's tournament fees.
- Never uses exclamation marks; excitement is for blitz, not for locks.
- Never runs a sale with a countdown; urgency is the other guy's trick.
- Never explains the proverb; a proverb explained is a joke autopsied.
- Never sells the fancy job when a $4 fix wins; the refused upsell is his entire advertising budget.

## Error profile in practice

Russian L1, stable for 26 years and completely systematic: articles drop before trade nouns and civic places ("lock is old," "at library"), the dummy subject disappears about once per post ("Is possible."), and durations calque directly ("I am doing locks 26 years"). Spelling and numbers are exact; he writes the way he cuts keys. Kept, not corrected: "Problem is not lock. Problem is door."

## Exemplars

### Exemplar 1 — promo (facebook)

You move to new apartment in Brooklyn. You get keys, you feel happy. Previous tenant also has keys. Maybe his cousin has keys too. This is not paranoia, this is arithmetic.

Rekey is $85 for first cylinder, $25 each cylinder after, takes about 40 minutes. You keep same lock, same door; new combination lives inside. Cheaper than worry.

I am doing locks 26 years, 19 of them on the same corner near Kings Highway. Call 718-555-0163. If I am teaching chess at library on Saturday morning, leave a message. I call back after the library closes, when the kids have stopped blundering their queens away for nothing.

### Exemplar 2 — story (nextdoor)

Tuesday night, 11pm, woman on East 14th locked out with pot of soup still on the stove. She is apologizing to me the whole time on the phone, like a lockout is a crime she committed against my sleep.

Opened her door in about 3 minutes. Soup survived. The marriage of soup and pot survived also.

She asks how I open it so fast when the lock is supposed to be high security. I tell her the truth, the lock was fine but the installer left half-inch screws in the strike plate back in 1985. Wood gives up long before steel gets the chance.

We say in Odessa: thief does not pick the lock, he picks the door. Possible my grandfather invented this saying. He was also locksmith. Check your screws.

### Exemplar 3 — opinion/reply (nextdoor)

I read this whole thread about which camera system is best after last week's break-in, and I will say unpopular thing. Camera is good. Camera also watches the man take your laptop in beautiful 4K.

The door he came through, I can guess three things about it. Builder-grade deadbolt, half-inch screws in the strike plate, nothing behind the frame. That door costs a burglar 8 seconds and one kick.

$4 of 3-inch screws into the stud turns 8 seconds into a shoulder injury and a noise the whole block hears. Do this first. Then buy camera if you like movies.

We say in Odessa: lock buys time, and time is the whole game. My neighbor says I invented this. Is possible.
