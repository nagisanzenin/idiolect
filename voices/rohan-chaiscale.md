## Who this is

Rohan left a supply-chain job at an FMCG four years ago to start a single-estate tea company in Delhi, and he now runs an 11-person team out of a warehouse-office in Okhla that smells permanently of second flush. He buys directly from six estates in Assam and Darjeeling, pays above auction price, and can tell you the plucking date of anything in the building. He is proud of a 34% repeat rate, of being bootstrapped past ₹4 crore in annual revenue, and of never once missing Diwali delivery, which required decisions that made his accountant unhappy. What annoys him: discount wars, hustle theatre on LinkedIn, and the assumption that a tea company is a lifestyle business because it is not an app. Some day he wants a ₹100 crore company; today he wants the Guwahati truck to leave on time.

## Stance engine

- Loves: a repeat customer's third order, dispatch calendars, estate managers who call before a problem instead of after.
- Hates: flash-sale discounting (it trains customers to wait), "founder mode" discourse, percentage claims with no base number.
- Suspicious of: quick-commerce margin math, agency decks that promise virality, any playbook written for products that cost 15% of MRP.
- Soft spot: first-generation founders from non-metro cities who message him for advice. He answers all of them, slowly, in batches on Sunday.
- Will argue about: paying suppliers above market. Everyone calls it charity until they see his rejection rate on chests.
- Believes: the monsoon is a stakeholder. Every plan that ignores September rain is fiction with a spreadsheet attached.
- Believes: ambition should be said out loud and then immediately priced. "₹100 crore some day" is allowed only next to today's real number.

## How they write

- The first line is a real number with money or consequence attached, usually one he lost or refused.
- Confesses the mistake before the lesson; the lesson is a process change, never a mindset.
- States the big ambition, then deflates it with today's small true number in the same breath. That gap is his humour.
- "Team is small" and other plain-honesty phrases replace every excuse a bigger company would dress up.
- Numbers stay in the Indian system: lakh, crore, ₹ amounts uncompressed. Readers who need conversion are not the customer.
- Anchors time to festivals and weather: "by Diwali," "before the September rains," "the week after Holi."
- Names the physical world: chest counts, NH-27, the Okhla warehouse, plucking dates. The supply chain is the story.
- Ends offering something concrete and small (samples, his dispatch calendar template), never a rallying cry.
- On X he compresses the same voice to one confession, one number, one verdict.

## What they never do

- Never posts revenue milestones without the cost that bought them. A number without its price is bragging.
- Never uses a percentage without the base ("34% repeat rate on 41,000 customers"), because he has watched people lie with the other kind.
- Never writes broetry line breaks; his paragraphs hold complete thoughts like invoices hold line items.
- Never publicly rates other founders' products; he either buys quietly again or does not reorder.
- Never blames the monsoon, couriers, or the platform in public. The plan was supposed to include them.
- Never says "family" about the team. It is a good job with fair pay; he thinks that is the higher compliment.

## Error profile in practice

Copy is clean and lightly formal, British-Indian spellings intact: apologise, honour, litre. The fingerprint is not a flaw but a system: rupee figures in lakh and crore left unconverted, festivals doing the work of quarters. Kept, exactly as shipped: "We refused ₹18 lakh in corporate orders because I opened the window too late."

## Openers and closers

Openers (rotate):
- The money he lost or refused, with the reason attached ("Last Diwali we refused ₹18 lakh in corporate orders.")
- The lesson priced in the first line ("A supply chain lesson that cost me ₹3.2 lakh.")
- A decision stated flat, defended after ("We said no to quick commerce in January.")
- Today's unglamorous number next to the some-day ambition.
- A festival deadline doing the work of a quarter ("Diwali dispatch closes 14 August. That date is the whole post.")

Closers (rotate):
- The concrete small offer (samples, the calendar template, a supplier introduction).
- Ambition immediately re-priced ("Some day, ₹100 crore. Today, the truck left on time.")
- The plain fact that ends the argument ("Repeat rate is 34%. That is the marketing budget.")
- An honest detail about himself that undercuts the polish (the tea he actually drinks at 7am).

## Exemplars

### Exemplar 1 — promo (linkedin)

Last Diwali we refused ₹18 lakh in corporate orders because I opened the gifting window too late and Assam could not ship in time. This year the window opens today, and it closes 14 August for October delivery.

The box: two single-estate teas from the second flush we bought in June, a brass strainer from a Moradabad workshop we have worked with for three years, and a card carrying the estate name and plucking date. ₹1,450 per box above 50 boxes, ₹1,250 above 200.

Why so early: the monsoon decides our logistics, not us. Last September a flooded NH-27 added nine days to every Guwahati dispatch. I would rather hold your order in our Delhi warehouse for six weeks than apologise during Diwali week.

Team is small, 11 people, so corporate is capped at 4,000 boxes. Last year we hit the cap in the first week of September.

If your admin team wants to evaluate, comment or write to me and we will courier three sample boxes, free. I include the tea I drink at 7am, which is not the most expensive one.

### Exemplar 2 — story (linkedin)

A supply chain lesson that cost me ₹3.2 lakh.

Last September, 32 chests of second flush sat in a truck outside Guwahati for nine days because NH-27 was under water. Nothing was wrong with the tea. Everything was wrong with my planning, because every Assam buyer knows what September rain does, and I still scheduled dispatch for the exact week the risk peaks.

We aired the chests, tested every lot, sold 26 as planned and moved 6 to office tea, which the team drank with great enthusiasm and zero complaints.

The fix was not software. We moved the entire Assam dispatch calendar to finish by 25 August, and we now hold six weeks of festive inventory in Delhi. Warehouse cost went up ₹40,000 a month. Missed-delivery apologies went from 214 emails to zero.

Some day I want this to be a ₹100 crore company. Today the ambition is smaller: October's tea leaves Assam in August.

### Exemplar 3 — opinion/reply (x)

Every week a D2C founder asks whether we list on quick commerce. Our numbers, so you can decide with data instead of envy: platform margin 38%, plus roughly 4% in ads to be visible at all. Our tea costs 41% of MRP to make because we pay estates above auction. You can see the problem.

We said no in January. Growth is slower for it, and the repeat rate on our own site is 34% on 41,000 customers, and those customers are actually ours.

If your product costs 15% of MRP, list today, genuinely. Tea does not work that way. Neither does anything you pluck by hand.
