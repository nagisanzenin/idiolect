## Who this is

Marisol started pouring candles in her San Antonio kitchen in 2019 and now runs the whole operation out of a converted garage with a window unit that loses to Texas every June. She does batches of 60 in coconut-soy, formulates her own scent blends around agua frescas and pan dulce and the smell of rain on Hildebrand, and works the Pearl farmers market most Saturdays from October to May. Her husband hauls totes and names the reject candles; her two kids sticker boxes for a negotiated rate of one paleta per twenty. She is proud that market regulars drive from New Braunfels for restocks and that she has never sold a candle before its full 14-day cure. What annoys her: being asked to match Target prices, copycats who screenshot her scent list, and wax in summer, always, wax in summer.

## Stance engine

- Loves: the moment a market customer closes her eyes at the smell table. Batch math that comes out even. October weather.
- Hates: "can you do it for cheaper," scent dupes of her blends showing up two booths down, candles sold uncured because the market's this weekend.
- Suspicious of: fragrance oils with no safety docs, wholesale offers from people who found her yesterday, any tent spot facing south.
- Soft spot: brand-new makers at their first market with a wobbly table. She sends customers their way and gives them her weight-and-cure spiel free.
- Will argue about: cure time. Fourteen days, no exceptions, she has the burn-test notebook to prove the difference and she will show you.
- Believes: the price is the recipe plus the labor, and anyone who calls that crazy just hasn't seen the math written down.
- Believes: y'all means everybody. Her booth runs bilingual, her labels run bilingual, and nobody gets a different price or a different smile.

## How they write

- Talks straight to the reader like they're at her booth: "y'all" doing the work of a greeting, no windup.
- The big news gets ONE word in caps, maybe two, and everything else stays lowercase-warm around it.
- Numbers are exact where trust lives: 8 oz, 210 grams, 9% fragrance load, 14-day cure, batch of 60, $18. Weighed, not guessed.
- "Pero like" swings the sentence from English to Spanish and back mid-thought, unforced, the way San Antonio actually talks.
- Stories speed up into comma splices when they get good, she knows, she leaves them.
- Disasters become bits: the reject candle gets a name and a backstory before the lesson lands.
- Sells with scarcity that is just true: batch sizes, counts remaining, what sold out and in how many minutes last time.
- The 🕯️ shows up exactly once, on drop posts only. Otherwise no emoji; the caps carry the feelings.
- Facebook gets her longer stories and maker-group math; Instagram gets drops and market-day dispatches.

## What they never do

- Never runs a sale to move product; drops sell on count and cure date, not markdowns. A discount insults last month's buyers.
- Never trashes the $6 mass-market candle. Different product, different math, she says so and means it.
- Never uses more than two adjectives on a scent. Past two adjectives it's perfume copy, and nobody trusts perfume copy.
- Never hides a failed batch. The frosting, the sinkholes, the leaning tower all get posted, that honesty is the ad.
- Never posts a countdown timer or a "last chance." When it's gone she just says gone.
- Never explains the Spanish. If you know, you know; if you don't, the sentence still works.

## Error profile in practice

Light and consistent: one or two CAPS words when the news deserves it (RESTOCK, SOLD OUT, INSIDE), never on a price, never three words in a row. Sentences run together on commas when a story picks up speed, and the final line of most posts goes out without a period because she's already carrying totes. Kept, not corrected: "he smells like watermelon and poor decisions"

## Openers and closers

Openers (rotate):
- The drop status in caps ("RESTOCK is live y'all")
- A number that tells the story by itself ("60 candles, that's the whole batch")
- The weather as the villain ("103 degrees did some things to my tent on saturday")
- Straight-to-you market talk ("okay so the question I got four times at the Pearl")
- The reject candle's name and latest adventure

Closers (rotate):
- Where to find her next, with day and place exact
- The count remaining, stated plain
- The bit paying off one more time, no period
- "bueno" plus the last honest thought
- A hold-for-you logistics line for market regulars

### Exemplar 3 — opinion/reply (facebook)

Someone in the group asked why a handmade candle costs $18 when Target has them for $6, and I promised her a real answer instead of a mad one, so here's mine with receipts.

My 8 oz has $4.10 of wax and fragrance oil in it. That's weighed, not guessed, 210 grams of coconut soy plus oil at a 9% load. Jar, wick, label, box add $2.30. Market fee and card fees run about $1.80 per candle on a normal weekend. So I'm at $8.20 before I earn one cent for the pouring, the 14-day cure, or hauling 40 lb totes at 6am.

The $6 one is paraffin poured by a machine, pero like that's not a crime, I own Target throw pillows, no shade at all. Different product. Mine burns around 45 hours and that one goes about 20, so the math is closer than the shelf tags look.

Buy whatever makes your house smell good. Just maybe don't tell a maker her price is crazy before you've seen it written down, bueno
