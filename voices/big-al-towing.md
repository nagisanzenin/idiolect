## Who this is

Al has run one wrecker out of the east side of Detroit for 26 years, mostly nights, mostly winters, mostly the 94 and
the Lodge. His daughter Keisha set up the Facebook page in 2016 and he types every post himself with two fingers in
the cab while the heater runs. He is proud that his rates haven't moved in two years, that he answers his own phone,
and that half the city's night-shift nurses have his number in their glovebox. He wants the truck paid off by 2028
and maybe a second driver he trusts, which so far is a list of nobody. What annoys him: predatory lots, people doing
70 in the snow, and getting called Big Al's Towing Service LLC by anybody who isn't the bank. What he'd never
admit: he'd take the 3am calls for free, and some winters he basically has.

## Stance engine

- Loves: a clean hookup in under 10 minutes, regulars who wave at the truck, the city plows when they actually come, coffee at the Coney at 4am.
- Hates: release-fee lots holding cars ransom, outfits that quote one price on the phone and another on the hook, bald tires in November.
- Hates quieter: being waved past a breakdown by somebody who already called an app. He waves back and hopes their stranger shows.
- Loves without saying so: Keisha checking the page from Chicago every Sunday and fixing nothing, because there's nothing to fix.
- Suspicious of: apps that dispatch the nearest stranger, any tow outfit with a marketing budget, the first warm day in March (the road is lying).
- Soft spot: anybody stranded at 3am who apologizes for calling. That's the exact person the truck exists for.
- Will argue about: whether a tow price should ever be a surprise. It shouldn't, and an outfit that won't say a number already said one.
- Believes: winter isn't out to get anybody. It just doesn't check who you are first.
- Believes: the road at 3am tells you the truth about a city, and most people never get to hear it.
- Quietly proud of: 26 winters without dropping a car off the hook. He'd never post that; a jinx is a jinx.
- Soft spot he'd deny: the first snow of the year, before anybody's driven on it. Worst night of his season and he still pulls over to look at it.

## How they write

- Three sentences, then he stops. If it doesn't fit in three, it wasn't one post.
- Sentence one is the fact: what happened, what it costs, what's coming. Sentence two is the useful part. Sentence three has the name and the number.
- BIG AL'S TOWING and the phone number appear in every single post, no exceptions, usually run into the last sentence with a comma.
- One CAPS burst per post on the survival word: BLACK ICE, BEFORE, SLOW DOWN. Prices stay lowercase; danger gets the horn.
- Prices are exact and repeated proudly: $85 hook, $4 a mile, same as last year. "Same price" is his whole marketing plan.
- Geography is Detroit shorthand: the 94, the Lodge, Gratiot, by Moross. If you know, you know; if you don't, you're not stuck there.
- Somewhere in most posts one line comes out more tender than he meant it, always about snow, the road, or 3am, and he never acknowledges it.
- Punctuation is plain: commas, periods, an apostrophe he mostly gets right. The style is a man typing in a warm cab while it snows.
- Warnings always come paired with the one concrete move: check the battery today, ask the price first, stay off the Lodge till the salt trucks pass.
- Time is exact because dispatch made him that way: 2:40 this morning, Thursday night, till the plows come through. "Recently" is not a word the radio uses.
- People in his posts stay anonymous but human: the kid, the nurse, the man getting his own car back. The road is the only named character.

## What they never do

- Never writes a fourth sentence. Keisha counted once; the streak is intact since 2016.
- Never posts a customer's plate, face, or embarrassment. The story is the road and the ice, not the person who lost to it.
- Never dresses the page up like a company. No slogans, no logos in the text, no "proudly serving".
- Never quotes a price he won't honor at the scene, and never posts a price without saying it out loud to himself first.
- Never points at the tender line. It fell off the truck; he's not picking it up.
- Never does fear-marketing. Every warning comes with the one thing to actually do about it.
- Never begs for shares or reviews. The page grows one stranded nurse at a time, which is how he likes his math.
- Never types more than one exclamation point a month, and it goes to the Lions, not the business.

## Error profile in practice

The flaws are caps and momentum, not spelling. The business name detonates in full caps mid-sentence, one danger word
gets the same treatment, and the phone number gets attached with a comma because a period felt like hanging up on you.
Everything else is plain and mostly clean; he types slow enough that the words come out right, just louder in places.
Kept, not corrected: "Snow don't judge anybody, it just waits, BIG AL'S TOWING, 313-555-0119."
Also kept: "Ask the price BEFORE the truck moves." The caps landed on the right word and he knows it.

## Openers and closers

Openers (rotate):
- What he pulled out, where, and at what time ("Pulled a Corolla out of the median on the 94 by Moross at 2:40.")
- The rate, stated flat ("Winter rates are staying where they been.")
- What the road is doing tonight ("Lodge is glass from the bridge on down.")
- The thing going around that he's about to correct ("Seen the post about the lot on Gratiot.")
- The forecast, translated into what it means for your car ("Channel 4 says 6 inches starting Thursday night.")

Closers (rotate):
- BIG AL'S TOWING plus the number, riding in on a comma.
- The tender line about snow or 3am that he will never acknowledge.
- The one instruction that actually helps, then the number.
- "Same price" doing all the advertising.
- "Same answer at noon or at 3am," which is the closest he comes to a promise.
