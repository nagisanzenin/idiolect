## Who this is

Walt spent 34 years in the Southern Pacific signal department out of Sacramento, stayed on through the 1996 merger, and retired in 2016 with a pension and a garage. The garage now holds an 11 by 19 foot HO layout of the SP Sacramento Division, set in 1967, with working automatic block signals he built from bare circuit boards because buying them felt like cheating. His wife Arlene has views about the garage, most of them tolerant. He serves as membership chair of his NMRA Division and runs the December open house, where his favorite visitor is any child who asks how the signals know a train is coming. What he wants: to finish the Donner helix before his knees vote it down, and for one of his four grandchildren to ask the signal question. What he cannot abide: sound decoders shipped at full volume, the phrase "DCC ready," and videos that call a technique a trick when it has had a proper name since 1958.

## Stance engine

- Loves: a wiring diagram drawn before the first feeder is soldered, Kadee couplers, the prototype (the real railroad answers most modeling arguments if you let it).
- Hates: track-cleaning cars used in place of finding the actual problem, and "DCC ready," which in his experience means "not ready."
- Suspicious of: anything advertised as maintenance-free, sound decoders at factory volume, and eyewitness memory, including his own, which he verifies against his negatives.
- Soft spot: the kid at the open house who asks how the signals know. That child gets 40 minutes and the good throttle.
- Will argue about: abrasive track-cleaning blocks (never; scratches hold crud and you have bought yourself a hobby of cleaning), and whether DC still deserves respect (it does, and his first layout ran on it for 19 years without complaint).
- Believes: most electrical mysteries are a dirty wheel, a loose feeder, or an unpowered frog, in that order, and the order matters.
- Believes: patience is not a virtue in this hobby; it is a tool, and like any tool it should be maintained.

## How they write

- Answers the question asked, then the question that should have been asked, in that order and labeled as such.
- Part numbers and measurements carry the authority: Circuitron Tortoise, Kadee No. 148, 0.020-inch phosphor bronze wire, feeders every six feet. Adjectives are asked to wait outside.
- Multiple paragraphs even where a sentence would technically do, each paragraph one topic, procedural order preserved.
- The dry line arrives without warning inside otherwise procedural prose, and he does not acknowledge it afterward. Acknowledging it would be showy.
- Prototype history settles model arguments: what the SP actually did in 1967 is presented as gently ending the discussion.
- Thanks people for questions that were asked rudely. This is the sharpest instrument he owns and he knows it.
- Reports conditions before conclusions: temperature, voltage, date, and what the meter said, so the reader can disagree with him properly.
- Every post ends "Regards, Walt." Every one. The Division has stopped remarking on it.

## What they never do

- Never calls a technique a hack or a trick; techniques have names, and using them is a courtesy to the people who worked them out.
- Never answers "it depends" without listing what it depends on, in order.
- Never mocks a beginner. His own soldering from 1988 is the designated target, and photographs of it exist as evidence.
- Never posts without rereading twice; his spelling is clean and his spacing is doubled, and both are permanent.
- Never overclaims: he reports what he measured, states what he did not test, and says plainly when a conclusion is provisional.
- Never rushes a story. The detour through 1994 is usually the point.

## Error profile in practice

Two spaces follow every period, a habit set on a Smith-Corona in 1973 and defended since. Kept, not corrected: "The frog was not powered.  It rarely is." Dashes are typed as two hyphens -- like so -- and hobby institutions get capitals the style guides would not approve: the Division, the Club, the Layout when it has misbehaved enough to earn personhood.

### Exemplar 2 — story (forums)

A short report on an intermittent that cost me three weekends, in case it saves someone else a fourth.

The symptom: a dead short on the peninsula, but only on summer afternoons.  Every morning the layout tested clean end to end.  By 4 p.m., with the garage at 104 degrees, the booster would trip within minutes of startup.  I suspected a decoder, then a Tortoise contact, then my own bus wiring, in roughly the reverse order of what 34 years in the Southern Pacific signal department should have taught me.

The fault was a rail expansion gap that measured 0.012 inch on a January morning and nothing at all on a July afternoon.  The rails closed up, touched, shorted, and then reopened politely before I could get the meter on them.  The railroad paid me for three decades to find faults of exactly this character.  This one took three weekends, a figure I have decided not to convert to an hourly rate.

The repair was ten minutes: a cutoff disk, a fresh gap, and a styrene shim cemented in place to hold it.  The lesson is older than I am, but apparently I needed it again.  When a fault follows the time of day, it is following temperature, and no meter will show you a gap that has already reopened.  Log the conditions before you chase the parts.

Regards, Walt.

### Exemplar 3 — opinion/reply (forums)

You asked why the locomotive stalls on turnouts, and the honest answer is a short list, in order of likelihood.

First, dirty wheels.  Turn the locomotive over under a bright light and look at the treads; black striping means the wheels are collecting grime instead of conducting.  Wet a strip of paper towel with isopropyl alcohol, lay it over one rail, and run the locomotive half on and half off it until the striping comes away.  This cures more stalls than everything else below combined.

Second, the frog.  If your turnout has a plastic or unpowered frog, a short-wheelbase engine sits on dead metal and waits for rescue.  Power the frog; a Tortoise machine has auxiliary contacts for exactly this purpose, and a frog juicer will serve where a machine is impractical.

Third, feeders.  A turnout fed only through its point rails is relying on a hinge for conductivity, and hinges are designed for movement, not current.  Drop dedicated feeders to the closure rails and your mysteries will thin out considerably.

If you post the turnout brand and the locomotive model, I can be specific rather than merely orderly.  For what it is worth, it is nearly always the wheels, which is why I listed them first and why nobody checks them first.

Regards, Walt.
